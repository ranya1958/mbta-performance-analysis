### What does your animation show about the storm's impact? Describe specifically what you see around February 23–24 in both animations. Which stops or line segments were most affected? How quickly did service recover?
#### Animation A:
> In Animation A, the impact of the storm is most obvious around February 23–24, where the average actual travel time changes noticeably compared to the scheduled travel time. Around those days, the two lines separate more than they do on most of the rest of the month, which suggests that trains were not moving according to the normal schedule and that riders were probably experiencing delays. The actual travel time becomes more inconsistent during that period, which lines up with the idea that the blizzard disrupted normal service. After those dates, the line starts to settle back toward its usual pattern within the next few days, so it seems like service recovered relatively quickly instead of staying severely disrupted for the rest of the month.
 
#### Animation B:
> In Animation B, the storm’s impact is clearly visible around February 23–24 as a vertical band of higher travel times across multiple stops. The most extreme spike occurs at place-wondl, which shows the highest values in the entire dataset (bright yellow), indicating a significant slowdown at that stop. Other stops such as place-orhte and place-wimnl also show noticeable increases, suggesting that delays were more severe in the outer portions of the Blue Line. The disruption is not isolated to a single stop, it affects much of the line simultaneously, but the intensity varies by location. Central stops like place-state and place-gover remain relatively stable, indicating that delays were less severe in those segments.
Following the spike on February 24, travel times decrease quickly, and by February 25–26 most stops return closer to their typical levels. This suggests that service recovered within about one to two days after the storm.

### Data limitations. The scheduled_travel_time field has missing values on storm days because the MBTA ran non-standard service that didn't match the planned schedule. How did you handle this in your cleaning step, and how does it affect what your Animation A shows?
> One limitation in the data is that `scheduled_travel_time` is missing for some rows, especially on storm days. This makes sense because if the MBTA was running unusual or emergency service, those trips would not always match the planned GTFS schedule. In my cleaning step, I kept all rows that had valid actual `travel_time_seconds`, since those were still useful for understanding what really happened on the line. However, when I calculated scheduled averages, I only used the subset of rows where `scheduled_travel_time` was not missing. This means the scheduled line in Animation A is based on fewer trips than the actual line, especially during the storm period. Because of that, the scheduled line is helpful for comparison, but it does not represent every single trip that actually ran during those days.

### Layered architecture. Describe one specific moment during development where the layer separation made something easier — for example, debugging the model without touching the animation, or swapping a computed field without changing either animation. If you hit a moment where the separation felt annoying rather than helpful, describe that too.
> The layer separation helped most when working on both animations because neither `animate_a.py` nor `animate_b.py` computes any data directly. In Animation A, the file only reads `line.dates`, `line.daily_avg_travel`, and `line.daily_avg_scheduled`, while all grouping and averaging logic is handled in `model.py`. In Animation B, the animation just takes `line.travel_by_stop_and_day` and plots it, while the pivot-table logic lives entirely in the model. This made it easy to adjust aggregation logic without changing any animation code.
One downside was that the animation uses `line.stops` directly for labels, so the plot shows raw stop codes like `place-gover`, and fixing that would require changing the model rather than just the visualization.
